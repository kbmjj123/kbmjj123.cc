Quitting the 9-to-5 to build my own products. The real struggles with product-market fit, tech choices, and mental health. Honest reflections for fellow builders.

## The Decision to Go Indie

It started with a simple realization: I was spending more time building side projects than my actual job. The passion projects, the late-night coding sessions, the dopamine hit of shipping something _you_ own — it wasn't a hobby anymore, it was a calling.

> "The best time to start is when you can't stop thinking about it."

Leaving a stable paycheck was terrifying. I saved six months of runway, negotiated a part-time consulting retainer, and jumped.

## First 3 Months: The Honeymoon

The first three months were pure bliss. I woke up when I wanted, coded what I wanted, and felt like I'd unlocked life on easy mode. I built three mini-projects in rapid succession:

- A CLI tool for quick project scaffolding
- A dashboard for tracking GitHub stars
- A minimal CMS for blogging

None of them had paying users. But they were fun.

### The Productivity Trap

Looking back, I was mistaking _activity_ for _progress_. Building things is easy. Building things people will pay for is hard. I was optimizing for the wrong metric: code output instead of customer value.

## Finding Real Pain Points

Month four hit different. Savings were draining. The fun projects weren't generating revenue. I started talking to potential customers — real conversations, not surveys.

What I learned:

1. Everyone wants "more time"
2. No one will pay for a tool that saves 5 minutes
3. People pay _a lot_ for tools that save them from embarrassment or legal risk

This shifted my entire approach. Instead of scratching my own itch, I started looking for _urgent_ itches in other people's workflows.

## Building the MVP

The MVP took six weeks. I set hard constraints:

- No custom backend — use existing APIs
- No authentication system — invite-only
- One core workflow only
- Ugly but functional UI

### The Pivot

Two weeks in, I realized the initial feature set was wrong. One user told me, "I'd use this if it did X instead of Y." I pivoted overnight. Saved by not over-investing in the wrong direction.

## Launch and Silence

Launch day came. I posted on Hacker News, Reddit, Twitter, and three niche communities.

The result: 47 visitors, 3 sign-ups, 0 paying customers.

### The Emotional Crash

I won't sugarcoat it — the silence after launch is brutal. You spend months building something, and the world shrugs. It took me a week to recover and start analyzing _why_ those 3 people signed up but didn't convert.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Talk to users before writing code** — at least 20 conversations
2. **Ship ugly** — beauty comes after retention
3. **Price early** — if you can't charge, you don't have a product
4. **Ignore advice from people who haven't built** — everyone has an opinion
5. **Prepare for the emotional rollercoaster** — it's real

### The Metric That Matters

The only metric that matters in year one: **are _any_ users begging you not to shut down?** If yes, you have something. Iterate. If no, keep talking to people.

## What's Next

I'm now on version 4 of the product. Revenue covers ramen. Three paying customers who'd be upset if I shut down. It's not a unicorn, but it's _real_.

The journey continues. More lessons to come.
