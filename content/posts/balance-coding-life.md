Indie devs are prone to burnout. I've developed a rhythm that boosts both productivity and well-being. Here's my practical framework to stay sustainable.

## Why Balance Matters

In the first year of my indie journey, I hit burnout twice. Not the "I'm tired" kind — the "can't look at a text editor without feeling nauseous" kind. I learned the hard way that coding 12 hours a day isn't sustainable.

> Burnout isn't a badge of honor. It's a sign your system is broken.

## Principle 1: Time Boxing

I divide my day into three blocks:

- **Morning (6-10 AM)**: Deep work, no meetings, no social media
- **Midday (10 AM-2 PM)**: Code reviews, emails, lighter tasks
- **Afternoon (2-5 PM)**: Meetings, content, community

Each block is a hard boundary. When the block ends, I switch tasks. No exceptions.

### The Science Behind It

Context switching is expensive. Research shows it takes **23 minutes** to regain focus after a distraction. Time boxing minimizes context switching by batching similar activities.

## Principle 2: Physical First

I made a rule: no coding until I've done at least 20 minutes of movement. Walk, stretch, gym — doesn't matter. Physical activity primes the brain for focus.

Consequences of skipping:

- **Day without movement**: mental fog by 2 PM
- **Week without movement**: mood swings, poor decisions
- **Month without movement**: guaranteed burnout

Your body is the only computer you can't restart. Treat it well.

## Principle 3: Deep Work Mornings

The first 4 hours of my day are sacred. No Slack, no email, no Twitter. Just me and the hardest problem I need to solve.

Tips that work:

1. Prepare the task the night before — decide _exactly_ what you'll work on
2. Block all distractions — app blockers, Do Not Disturb, airplane mode
3. Work in 90-minute sprints with 15-minute breaks

### What Not to Do

Don't check "quick notifications" before deep work. That `⌘+Tab` to Slack destroys your focus window before it starts.

## Principle 4: No-Code Evenings

Evenings are for non-coding activities. Reading, cooking, spending time with family, gaming, or just staring at the ceiling.

The rule is simple: **after 6 PM, no work-related code**. Writing personal scripts for hobbies is fine — the key is that it's _play_, not _work_.

This separation is crucial for long-term sustainability.

## Principle 5: Weekly Review

Every Friday afternoon, I spend one hour reviewing the week:

1. What shipped?
2. What blocked me?
3. What drained my energy?
4. What energized me?
5. Adjust next week's plan

This practice prevents drift. Without it, weeks blur into months of going nowhere.

### The Review Question That Changed Everything

> "If I only had 20 hours next week, what would I work on?"

This question forces real prioritization. Most of what we do doesn't matter. Find what does and do that.
